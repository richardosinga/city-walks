South of Market was never supposed to be beautiful. For most of San Francisco's history it was the neighbourhood the rest of the city looked down on, literally and figuratively — a flat, foggy grid of warehouses, machine shops, flophouses, and workers' hotels crowded below the ridge of Market Street. That history is still visible if you know where to look, and this walk traces it from the museums that now anchor the neighbourhood's western edge to the restored ferry terminal on the waterfront where the city first met the Bay.

Start at **SF MOMA**, 151 Third Street. The building itself deserves a moment of attention before you go inside. The original structure was completed in 1995, designed by the Swiss architect Mario Botta — a cylindrical skylight in striped black and white granite that became one of the defining images of the 1990s museum-building boom. In 2016 the museum added an expansion designed by the Norwegian firm Snøhetta that more than doubled the gallery space and clad the new facade in a rippling white fiberglass skin meant to evoke the fog and light of the Bay. The living wall on the Natoma Street side — 19,000 plants, 21 species — has become one of the most photographed surfaces in a neighbourhood that was not previously known for vegetation.

The collection inside contains one of the strongest holdings of postwar American art in the country, assembled partly through decades of gifts from Bay Area collectors with deep pockets and strong opinions. Richard Serra, Diane Arbus, Dorothea Lange, Wayne Thiebaud, and a substantial photography collection occupy the upper floors. The Calder room, with its ceiling of hanging mobiles, is worth the price of admission on its own. Allow ninety minutes if you intend to see it properly; allow three hours if you intend to get lost in it.

When you are done, cross **Howard Street** to **Yerba Buena Gardens**. The complex here — gardens, children's museum, theatre, and convention centre — represents the outcome of one of the more consequential urban planning battles in San Francisco's history. Through the 1970s and 1980s, the city demolished hundreds of buildings in this zone and displaced thousands of low-income residents to build the **Moscone Convention Center**, which opened underground in 1981. Community groups that had been pushed out fought back through the courts for years, eventually winning the gardens and cultural facilities above as a condition of continued expansion. The result is an unusual kind of public space: manicured and civic on the surface, carrying the memory of displacement in every blade of grass.

The **Martin Luther King Jr. Memorial** on the east side of the gardens is a fifty-foot waterfall pouring over a curved granite wall. Behind the falls, etched into the stone, are passages from King's speeches in a dozen languages. The spray carries across the path on a windy day. The **Children's Creativity Museum**, just north, occupies a former carousel pavilion and runs an animation and digital arts programme that has been one of the more durable cultural institutions in the neighbourhood. The restored historic Looff carousel out front dates from 1906.

Walk north on **Third Street** toward Market. Third runs through what used to be the heart of SoMa's printing and publishing district; the street was lined with print shops and binderies through the first half of the twentieth century, and the buildings that remain tend to have deep floor plates and oversized freight doors that give away their industrial origins. The **San Francisco Chronicle** occupied its building at 901 Mission Street, on the corner with Fifth, from 1924 until 2012. The Chronicle was the city's dominant newspaper for most of that century; its architecture — a severe, stone-faced block designed by George Kelham — reflects the self-confidence of an institution that expected to be there forever. It no longer publishes from this address, but the building remains.

Continue north to **Market Street**, the diagonal slash that cuts across the city's grid at an angle determined by the original Spanish survey of 1839. The intersection where Third, Kearny, and Geary all converge at Market is one of the most complicated in the city — a six-way crossing that has been confusing pedestrians and drivers alike for a hundred and fifty years. Look down at the centre of the intersection: the slight depression in the asphalt marks what old San Franciscans called **the Slot**, the trench that ran the length of Market Street carrying the cable that powered the original cable cars. The slot closed in 1906 when the cable system was torn up after the earthquake. The ghost of it remains in the street's profile.

Cross Market and enter the **Financial District**. The streets here — **Montgomery**, **Sansome**, **Battery** — run on a grid laid out in 1847 by Jasper O'Farrell, the city's first official surveyor. In the 1850s, during the Gold Rush, the waterfront stood at Montgomery Street; the blocks between Montgomery and the present shoreline are built entirely on fill, bay mud and rubble and debris, landfilled over the following decades as the city expanded east into the Bay. The foundations of the buildings you are walking past are driven through that fill into bedrock forty or fifty feet below the street. In 1906, when the earthquake liquefied the fill, whole blocks sank.

The **Transamerica Pyramid** at 600 Montgomery Street rises at the corner of Clay, a pointed white shaft 853 feet tall that has been the most recognisable element of the San Francisco skyline since it was completed in 1972. The architect was William Pereira. When it was built, local newspapers called it a monstrosity; within a decade it had become the symbol of the city. The redwood grove planted on the Hotaling Street side — 80 trees on the site of the old Montgomery Block, the 19th-century building where Mark Twain and Bret Harte once worked — provides a quiet moment in the middle of the Financial District if you need one.

Walk east through the financial streets toward the **Embarcadero**. The elevated freeway that once blocked views and light along this stretch was the Embarcadero Freeway, built in 1959 and demolished in 1991 after the Loma Prieta earthquake of 1989 damaged it beyond economic repair. Removing it opened up the waterfront for the first time in three decades and allowed the Embarcadero to be rebuilt as the boulevard it is now. San Francisco residents voted twice to keep the freeway and twice to remove it; the earthquake made the decision for them.

The **Ferry Building** stands at the foot of Market Street at 1 Ferry Building, its 245-foot clock tower rising above the waterfront. The building was completed in 1898, designed by A. Page Brown in the Beaux Arts style, with a barrel-vaulted nave running the length of the ground floor. For the first half of the twentieth century it was the busiest transit terminal on the Pacific Coast. Before the Bay Bridge opened in 1936, as many as 50,000 commuters passed through the Ferry Building every day, boarding and disembarking the boats that connected San Francisco to Oakland, Berkeley, Marin, and the East Bay. When the bridge opened, the ferries became redundant almost overnight. Passenger numbers collapsed. The building was walled off from the waterfront by the elevated Embarcadero Freeway in 1959 and fell into decades of quiet decline, its grand nave subdivided into offices and storage.

The restoration, completed in 2003, removed the internal partitions, reopened the Market Street arcade, and installed the marketplace that now occupies the nave. The Saturday farmers' market on the waterfront side has grown into one of the finest farmers' markets in California — a regular destination for professional chefs from across the Bay Area who come for the dry-farmed tomatoes, the Cowgirl Creamery cheeses, the Brokaw avocados, the stone fruit from Frog Hollow Farm. It runs from eight in the morning to two in the afternoon on Saturdays; Tuesday and Thursday markets are smaller but worth stopping at if you happen to be passing.

Stand at the eastern edge of the building and look out across the Bay. On a clear day you can see the East Bay hills, the Bay Bridge stretching toward Oakland, the cranes at the Port of Oakland on the far shore, Yerba Buena Island in the middle of the water, and — on the best days — Mount Diablo rising forty miles inland. The ferries still run from the terminal: regular service to Sausalito, Larkspur, Tiburon, Vallejo, and Oakland. You can board one here and be on the water in minutes, which is still the best way to understand what San Francisco actually looks like from the outside.

**SoMa** has changed character several times within living memory. In the first half of the twentieth century it was the city's industrial and warehouse district, home to the printing trades, the garment industry, and the light manufacturing that kept the port economy running. After the Second World War, as industry moved to the suburbs and the port containerised, the neighbourhood hollowed out. Through the 1970s and into the 1980s, the cheap warehouse space attracted a gay leather bar scene — the Folsom Street corridor, with its network of bars and clubs, was one of the most concentrated gay neighbourhoods in America before the AIDS crisis reshaped it. Then, in the 1990s, the first dot-com boom arrived. South of Market was the original location of what became the internet industry: startups crowded into converted warehouses along Folsom, Brannan, and Townsend, the cheap rents and large floor plates making it possible to put fifty engineers in a building that had previously stored wholesale plumbing fixtures. Most of those companies are gone. The second tech boom of the 2010s brought larger companies and higher rents. The neighbourhood is now a mixture of galleries, restaurants, tech offices, and residential buildings, with the industrial past surviving in the architecture if not always in the use.

The walk is approximately 2 km from SF MOMA to the Ferry Building and takes 2 to 3 hours at a comfortable pace, depending on how long you spend inside the museum and how thoroughly you stop at the Ferry Building market.
