The Mission was founded by soldiers and priests who wanted to pacify a continent. What they built instead was a neighbourhood that has never stopped arguing — with its colonisers, its landlords, its own changing demographics, and the city that keeps trying to reinvent it. This walk traces that argument from its beginning to its present: from the oldest building in San Francisco to the street where the Mission still insists on its own terms.

Start at **Mission Dolores**, the adobe chapel on Dolores Street where the city began. The building was completed in 1791 and has survived every earthquake since. Walk inside if it is open: the ceiling is painted in patterns derived from Coast Miwok basketry, and the cemetery beside it holds five thousand graves. The ornate basilica attached to it was added in 1918, a century after the Spanish empire that built the original had collapsed. Stand between the two buildings and you have the whole arc of the city's origins in a single glance.

Walk south down **Dolores Street** toward the park. The median strip running down the centre was planted with Canary Island palms in stages beginning in the 1920s, and they are now tall enough to give the street a genuinely boulevard character. The houses along either side are a textbook of San Francisco's Victorian and Edwardian residential architecture — flat-fronted Italianates, ornate Queen Annes, stick-style cottages, painted Edwardians — most of them built in the decade after the 1906 earthquake levelled the city and survivors rebuilt as fast as they could afford to.

**Dolores Park** opens up at 18th Street. Climb to the upper edge of the park for the view — downtown, the Bay, the Bay Bridge, Alcatraz, the hills of the East Bay running along the horizon. On a clear afternoon this is one of the finest urban views in California. The gold-painted fire hydrant at Church and 20th, just north of the park, marks where the 1906 fire stopped: the water pressure held here when it failed everywhere else, and Mission residents repaint the hydrant gold every year on 18 April, the earthquake's anniversary.

Leave the park and walk east along 18th Street. At the corner of Lapidge Street, stop and look up at the **Women's Building**. The five-storey mural MaestraPeace, painted in 1994 by seven Mission muralists, covers every exterior surface. A pregnant goddess with butterfly wings anchors the 18th Street face; the portrait of Nobel Peace Prize laureate Rigoberta Menchú looks down from the Lapidge side. The building has been a women's community centre since 1979; the mural has been stopping foot traffic since the day it went up.

Continue east on 18th Street to Valencia, turn left (north), and look for the entrance to **Clarion Alley** on the right after half a block. The alley runs one block between Valencia and Mission Streets and every surface is murals. The Clarion Alley Mural Project, founded here in 1992, operates on a principle of constant renewal: old murals get painted over, new ones respond to current events within weeks of them happening. This is not a museum. The alley argues back at the city in real time.

Return to Valencia Street and turn south. Walk down to 24th Street and turn left (east) on Mission Street. Walk south one more block to 24th and continue east: **Balmy Alley** is on the right, running between 24th and 25th Streets one block east of Mission. This is where the Mission mural movement began. In the mid-1980s, artists and activists angry about US military intervention in Central America painted every fence and garage door in the alley — the neighbourhood's walls became a form of international solidarity. About 33 murals now line Balmy, some from the 1980s, others recent. PRECITA EYES, the mural arts organisation whose studio is nearby on 24th Street, runs weekend tours of this alley and the surrounding blocks; they are the best guide to the history of what you are looking at.

Walk back west along 24th Street. This is **Calle 24**, the fourteen-block Latino cultural district whose commercial corridor has survived fifty years of gentrification pressure through a combination of cultural designation, community organising, and sheer stubbornness. The panadería that has been here for thirty years is still here. So is the carnecería, the botanica, the record shop, the competing taquerías. Stop at **Galería de la Raza** at number 2857, a storefront gallery founded in 1970 at the height of the Chicano civil rights movement. The gallery started the first community mural programme in the United States, introduced Frida Kahlo's work to American audiences, and has been running exhibitions in this building for over fifty years.

The walk ends at the **24th Street Mission BART station** at the intersection of 24th and Mission. The station plaza has been the site of housing protests, immigrant rights vigils, and neighbourhood celebrations since the station opened in 1973. The hummingbird railings and the tile mural at the entrance are worth a second look. Board here or, if the afternoon allows, keep walking west on 24th Street: Carnaval, the largest Latino cultural festival on the West Coast, fills these blocks every May, and on Día de los Muertos in November the street transforms into an outdoor altar that stretches for blocks.

The walk is approximately 3.5 km and takes about 2 to 2.5 hours at a comfortable pace, longer if you stop to read the murals or visit the gallery.
