Mission Dolores Park sits on two blocks of former Jewish cemetery land that the city converted to a park in 1905, a year before the earthquake. On a clear day — and the Mission, sheltered from the Pacific fog by Noe Valley's hills, gets more clear days than most of San Francisco — the park offers one of the city's most satisfying urban panoramas: the downtown skyline to the north, the Bay glinting behind it, the Castro's rainbow flag visible in the middle distance.

The park is a genuine community gathering place in a city that can feel segregated by neighbourhood and income. On weekend afternoons it fills with a cross-section of the Mission that is increasingly hard to find elsewhere: families with children from the playground at the eastern end, young professionals with wine and artisanal cheese on the sunny upper slope, longtime Latino residents with coolers and music, dogs off-leash on the lower lawn. The unofficial food vendors — elote carts, paleta sellers, the woman with the michelada stand — have been operating here for decades and are as much a part of the park as the grass.

The historic gold-painted fire hydrant at the intersection of Church and 20th Streets, a block from the park's northern entrance, marks where Mission residents made a stand during the 1906 fire. While the rest of the city burned, residents painted this hydrant gold to honour the water pressure that held here when it failed everywhere else. It is repainted every year on the earthquake's anniversary, 18 April.
