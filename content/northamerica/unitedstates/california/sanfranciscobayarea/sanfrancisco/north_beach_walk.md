North Beach sits at the foot of Telegraph Hill, wedged between the Financial District to the south, Fisherman's Wharf to the northwest, and the cliff face of the hill itself rising abruptly to the east. It was the neighbourhood of the Beat Generation, and before that of the Italian fishing community that gave it its character, and before that of the tent cities that spread here after the 1906 earthquake. This walk starts in its central square, dips into the literary geography of Columbus Avenue, then climbs the wooden staircases on the eastern face of Telegraph Hill to reach Coit Tower at the summit. The views from the top take in the entire bay.

Start at **Washington Square Park**, bounded by Columbus Avenue, Filbert Street, Union Street, and Stockton Street. The square is North Beach's living room: elderly Chinese residents practice tai chi on the grass in the mornings, families eat lunch on the benches, and the bocce courts on the south edge have been in use for decades. The bronze statue in the northwest corner depicts Benjamin Franklin; the one across the park is a memorial to volunteer firemen. Neither has any particular connection to North Beach, which is part of their charm. On the Columbus side of the square, the white spires of the Church of Saints Peter and Paul rise over the roofline — a 1924 Romanesque basilica where Joe DiMaggio had his wedding photographs taken in 1954, despite not being married here (he was divorced and the church refused the ceremony; the photos were taken on the steps regardless).

Walk south along Columbus Avenue. This is North Beach's main commercial spine, and it has not changed in character as dramatically as most San Francisco streets have: the cafés are still Italian in ancestry, the delis still hang salami in the windows, the air still smells of espresso. At the corner of Broadway you pass **Vesuvio Café**, a narrow bar that opened in 1948 and became one of the central gathering places of the Beat writers when City Lights opened across the alley. Jack Kerouac drank here; so did Dylan Thomas, Allen Ginsberg, and Lawrence Ferlinghetti. The interior is stacked with ephemera, photographs, and mismatched furniture on two floors; it functions today much as it always did.

Cross Jack Kerouac Alley, the narrow pedestrian lane running between Vesuvio and **City Lights Bookstore** at 261 Columbus Avenue. The alley was renamed in the poet's honour in 1988 and the pavement is inlaid with lines from Kerouac, Ferlinghetti, and Jaime Sabines. City Lights itself was founded in 1953 by Lawrence Ferlinghetti and Peter Martin as the first all-paperback bookstore in the United States. Three years later Ferlinghetti published Allen Ginsberg's *Howl and Other Poems* here, was arrested for obscenity, went to trial, and won — a landmark First Amendment case that effectively ended literary censorship in America. The bookstore is still independently owned and still sells poetry. Browse the basement poetry room before you leave; it is one of the best-stocked poetry sections in any bookstore anywhere.

Turn left (east) off Columbus onto **Green Street** and walk one block to Grant Avenue. Turn right (south) on Grant for half a block, then turn left onto **Vallejo Street**. Vallejo runs east toward the base of Telegraph Hill. Cross Kearny Street and look ahead: the hill rises steeply in front of you, and the route up is on foot. Turn right (south) on Kearny, walk one block to **Greenwich Street**, and turn left. Greenwich Street ends at a wooden staircase that climbs directly up the face of the hill. Take the stairs.

The **Greenwich Steps** ascend through one of the most unusual residential pockets in San Francisco — a cluster of cottages, gardens, and hidden lanes clinging to the east face of the hill where no vehicle can reach. The gardens here are the product of decades of volunteer planting: fig trees, lemon trees, fennel gone wild, flowers in every season. As you climb, the city drops away below you and the bay opens to the east. Pause on the landings and look out: the Ferry Building, the Bay Bridge, Alcatraz, the hills of Marin across the water.

Midway up the stairs, listen for the **wild parrots of Telegraph Hill**. The colony — several hundred cherry-headed conures, originally escaped or released pets — has lived on these cliffs since the 1990s and is now the subject of a documentary film and a book. The birds are loud, fast, and unmistakable: bright green with red caps, they flock through the trees in groups of a dozen or more, landing on the cliff faces and in the gardens along the steps. They are not shy. They nest in holes in the rock face and in the cavities of the old wooden structures on the hill. If you are here in the morning you are almost certain to see them.

At the top of the Greenwich Steps, turn left and follow the path a short distance to the summit. From the Greenwich approach you emerge close to the base of **Coit Tower**, the fluted concrete column that stands at the highest point of Telegraph Hill. The tower was built in 1933 with funds left by Lillie Hitchcock Coit, an eccentric San Francisco socialite who was devoted to the city's volunteer fire department and left a third of her estate to beautify the city. The tower was not designed to resemble a fire hose nozzle, despite what every tour guide says; it is simply a column.

Go inside. The lobby of Coit Tower contains one of the finest collections of Depression-era public art in California: a series of frescoes painted in 1934 by more than twenty artists under the Public Works of Art Project, the New Deal programme that employed artists during the Depression. The murals cover every wall of the ground floor and wrap around the stairwell. They depict California workers — longshoremen, factory hands, farmworkers, librarians, newsboys — in the Social Realist style that was the dominant mode of the period. Several panels include newspapers with headlines from 1934; others contain details that caused a scandal at the time, including hammer-and-sickle imagery that briefly delayed the tower's opening while city officials argued about whether to paint over it. Nothing was painted over. The murals have been restored and are as vivid now as they were when the scaffolding came down.

Take the elevator or the stairs to the observation platform at the top. The tower is not especially tall — Telegraph Hill itself does most of the work — but the position at the summit of a steep-sided hill in the middle of a flat city gives the view a quality out of proportion to the elevation. To the north, Fisherman's Wharf stretches along the waterfront and the headlands of Marin rise above the Golden Gate. To the east, the Bay Bridge spans toward Treasure Island and the East Bay hills. To the south, the downtown towers cluster together and the hills of the peninsula recede in the distance. To the west, the city extends toward Twin Peaks and Mount Sutro. On a clear day — and North Beach gets fewer of the afternoon fogs that blanket the Sunset and Richmond — this is a view worth sitting with.

Descend via the **Filbert Steps**, which drop steeply down the eastern face of the hill on the opposite side from the Greenwich approach. The Filbert Steps are a wooden boardwalk in their steepest section, threading through garden plots maintained by residents of the adjacent cottages. The gardens are intensely planted: roses, nasturtiums, succulents, fruit trees. At the base of the wooden section, the steps continue as concrete down to Sansome Street and the flat land at the bottom of the hill. Turn left on Sansome and walk north along the Embarcadero to the waterfront, or retrace your route back up to Columbus Avenue for coffee.

The walk is approximately 2.5 km and takes between one and a half and two hours at a comfortable pace, longer if you spend time in City Lights or linger over the murals inside Coit Tower.
