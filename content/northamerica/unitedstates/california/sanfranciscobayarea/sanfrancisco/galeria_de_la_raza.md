Founded in 1970 at the height of the Chicano civil rights movement, Galería de la Raza is one of the oldest continuously operating Chicano and Latino arts organisations in the United States. It has occupied its current address at 2857 24th Street since 1972, making it one of the anchor institutions of the Calle 24 cultural district.

The founding artists — who included Rupert García, Graciela Carrillo, and René Yañez — had a specific argument to make: that Chicano and Latino art was not folk art or decoration but a serious aesthetic and political practice that deserved the same institutional recognition as European and Anglo-American art. Galería started the first community mural programme in the United States, helped bring Frida Kahlo's work back to American audiences when it had fallen out of fashion, and ran programmes in printmaking, photography, and digital art that trained generations of Mission artists.

The gallery's street-facing storefront window has always been used as a mini-exhibition space visible from the sidewalk, a deliberate choice to make art accessible to people who might not enter a formal gallery. Inside, the exhibition programme continues to focus on Chicano, Latino, and Indigenous artists and on work that addresses social and political conditions. The building sits in the heart of 24th Street's commercial corridor, surrounded by the taquerías, panaderías, and community organisations that have made this block what Calle 24 calls "El Corazón de la Misión" — the heart of the Mission.
