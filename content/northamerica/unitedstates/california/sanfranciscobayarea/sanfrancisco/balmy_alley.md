A residential alley one block east of Mission Street between 24th and 25th Streets, and the origin point of the Mission mural movement. The first murals appeared here in 1972, painted by artists connected to the Galería de la Raza. In the mid-1980s, a collective of artists and activists including Patricia Rodriguez and Miranda Bergman covered the alley's fences and garage doors with murals responding to US military intervention in Central America — a conflict that was filling the Mission with Salvadoran, Guatemalan, and Nicaraguan refugees who had nowhere else to go.

The alley now holds approximately 33 murals in various states of preservation and renewal. Some of the originals from the 1980s have been carefully maintained. Others have been repainted, replaced, or built upon. The themes have expanded from Central American solidarity to cover gentrification, immigration enforcement, environmental justice, and the general condition of living in a neighbourhood that the rest of the city keeps trying to turn into something more profitable.

PRECITA EYES, the Mission mural arts organisation founded in 1977 by Susan Kelk Cervantes, leads walking tours of Balmy Alley and the surrounding blocks on weekends. Their studio is a few blocks away on 24th Street and they are the best single resource for understanding the mural history of the neighbourhood. The alley itself is always accessible; the murals are best seen in morning or late-afternoon light.
