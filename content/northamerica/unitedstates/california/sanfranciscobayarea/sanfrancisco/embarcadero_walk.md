San Francisco was built on its waterfront and then, for most of the twentieth century, walled itself off from it. The Embarcadero Freeway, an elevated double-deck highway completed in 1959, cut the city off from the Bay for thirty years, casting the piers into permanent shade and killing the street life along the waterfront. The 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake damaged the freeway enough that the city voted to tear it down. By 1991 it was gone, and the Embarcadero was open again — a broad boulevard running the full arc of the waterfront from Fisherman's Wharf in the northwest to the Bay Bridge in the east. This walk covers the northern half of that arc, from the old fishing docks to the science museum in the converted ferry terminal at Pier 15.

Start at **Fisherman's Wharf**, at the foot of Taylor Street where it meets Jefferson. The wharf has been San Francisco's working fishing harbour since Italian immigrant fishermen — most of them from Sicilian villages around Palermo — established it in the 1850s. They came to a city that had just gone berserk with gold rush money, and they fed it. The boats they used were feluccas, the lateen-rigged Mediterranean fishing vessels they knew from home, and on foggy mornings the bay was full of their red sails. By the early twentieth century steam had replaced sail and the Sicilian families had built a permanent industry here, with their own processing operations, their own restaurants, their own neighbourhood identity.

The tourist apparatus around the wharf today can make it easy to miss the fact that it is still a working port. Walk to the end of the fish piers, past the crab pots and the Dungeness crab season signs, and you will find the actual fishing boats — the F/V boats, their names lettered on their transoms, their gear on deck. The crab boats go out before dawn from November through June. Jefferson Street behind you is given over to souvenir shops and chain restaurants, but the smell of brine and diesel at the pier end is the real thing.

Walk west two blocks to **Ghirardelli Square** at 900 North Point Street. The red-brick complex occupying the block between North Point, Polk, Beach, and Larkin streets is one of the founding examples of adaptive reuse in American architecture. Domingo Ghirardelli, a Genoese confectioner who arrived in San Francisco during the gold rush, built his chocolate manufacturing complex here between 1893 and 1915. The buildings are a catalogue of late Victorian industrial architecture — the Clock Tower, the Woolen Building, the Cocoa Building, the Mustard Building — each named for what it originally housed. By the 1960s the factory had moved to larger premises and the complex was scheduled for demolition. Instead, developers William Roth and his mother Lurline Matson Roth bought it in 1962 and converted it into shops and restaurants, opening in 1964. It was one of the first projects of its kind in the United States, predating the transformation of Faneuil Hall in Boston and the South Street Seaport in New York by more than a decade, and it established a template for historic waterfront redevelopment that cities across the country would follow.

From Ghirardelli, walk one block east along Beach Street to **Hyde Street Pier**, the southern entrance to the San Francisco Maritime National Historical Park. The National Park Service maintains a fleet of historic vessels moored here, and they are among the finest examples of working maritime heritage anywhere on the West Coast. The **Balclutha**, a 256-foot, three-masted square-rigger built in Glasgow in 1886 of Scottish steel, carried Cape Horn cargo — grain, coal, case oil — for thirty years before ending her working life in the Alaska salmon trade. She is open to walk through, and standing on her main deck with the Bay behind you and her rigging above gives a clear sense of the scale of nineteenth-century oceanic commerce. The **C.A. Thayer**, a three-masted lumber schooner built in 1895, worked the Pacific coastal lumber trade for decades and later served the Alaska codfish fleet. The park also maintains a steam ferryboat, a paddle-wheel tugboat, and a World War II submarine. Admission covers all the vessels.

Walk east from the pier along the waterfront path. The shoreline here was aggressively reclaimed from the Bay in the late nineteenth century: the original shore was well inland of where you are standing, and the current waterfront was built on landfill between the 1850s and the 1900s. The piers you pass are numbered: the numbering system for San Francisco piers runs from 1 at the foot of Market Street up to 45 at Fisherman's Wharf, with odd numbers on the north side of Market and even on the south. The piers themselves are engineering artifacts — their timber substructure was driven into Bay mud a century ago and much of it is still there, albeit in varying states of integrity.

**Pier 39** is at the eastern edge of Fisherman's Wharf, reached from Beach Street or from the waterfront path. The pier was built as a tourist attraction in 1978, converting a derelict cargo pier into a two-level shopping and entertainment complex. It has been relentlessly commercial from the start, but something happened here in January 1990 that gave it a permanent claim on the city's affections: in the weeks after the Loma Prieta earthquake, California sea lions began hauling out on the floating docks on the pier's west side. By the end of the winter there were several hundred of them; by the following year the colony had grown to over a thousand. The pier's operators eventually gave over the entire west marina to the animals, removing the boats that had been docked there. The sea lions have been a permanent fixture ever since, sunning themselves on the docks, barking at each other in rotating shifts, occasionally tumbling into the water in a confusion of flippers. The Marine Mammal Center on the Marin Headlands provides information boards near the docks explaining the colony's history and behaviour. The best view is from the northwest corner of the pier's lower level.

Continue east along The Embarcadero. The boulevard is wide here, with the historic piers on the Bay side and a mix of office buildings and converted warehouses on the landward side. The **Bay Bridge** becomes visible as you walk south — its western span, the double-deck suspension bridge built in 1936, occupies the view ahead and to your right. After the Loma Prieta earthquake severely damaged its eastern span, the bridge underwent a decade-long eastern rebuild completed in 2013, replacing the old truss section with a single-tower self-anchored suspension span that is now illuminated at night by the Bay Lights installation, 25,000 white LED lights programmed to display shifting patterns across the cables. From the Embarcadero in the early evening, with the bridge lit and the Bay going dark behind it, the view is one of the best in the city.

**Pier 15**, reached by walking south along The Embarcadero from Pier 39, is the home of the **Exploratorium**. Frank Oppenheimer — physicist, brother of J. Robert Oppenheimer, and a man who had been blacklisted from academic physics during the McCarthy era — founded the Exploratorium in 1969 in the old Palace of Fine Arts building in the Marina district. His idea was a museum of science and perception in which every exhibit was interactive: you learn by doing, by touching, by experimenting, not by reading labels on glass cases. The museum he built became one of the most influential science education institutions in the world, directly inspiring the design of science centres on every continent. In 2013 the Exploratorium moved to its current location in the historic Ferry Building complex at Pier 15, a building constructed in 1896 as a terminal for the bay ferries that served as San Francisco's primary connection to the East Bay before the bridges were built. The renovation is sympathetic: the building's industrial bones — its long shed structure, its steel trusses, the open volume of the nave — are preserved and integrated into the museum's design. The Exploratorium's exhibits on wave patterns, light and colour, perception, and the physics of everyday phenomena work particularly well against the backdrop of the Bay visible through the building's windows and from its outdoor terrace.

If time and energy allow, **the Ferry Building** itself is ten minutes further south along The Embarcadero, at the foot of Market Street. The building's famous clock tower, modelled on the Giralda in Seville, stood for decades as the first landmark arriving ferry passengers saw as they crossed the Bay. The Saturday farmers' market in the arcade outside is one of the finest in California. The interior has been converted to a market hall of food vendors and specialty shops, with the original barrel-vaulted nave preserved at its full length.

The walk from Fisherman's Wharf to the Exploratorium is approximately 3 kilometres and takes two to three hours at an easy pace, longer with stops inside the Maritime Park vessels or the Exploratorium. The waterfront path is flat, uninterrupted, and well-maintained for its entire length.
