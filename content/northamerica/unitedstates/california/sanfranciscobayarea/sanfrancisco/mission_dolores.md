The oldest intact building in San Francisco, and the reason the neighbourhood exists. Mission San Francisco de Asís — universally known as Mission Dolores, after the creek that once ran nearby — was founded on 29 June 1776, just five days before the American Declaration of Independence was signed on the other side of the continent. The adobe walls of the chapel were completed in 1791 and have stood through every earthquake since, including the catastrophic one of 1906 that levelled most of the city around it.

The building is small and surprisingly plain: thick whitewashed adobe, a tile roof, a facade of paired columns that is more functional than ornamental. Inside, the ceiling is painted in a chevron pattern derived from Coast Miwok basket designs — the labour of the Indigenous people who built the mission under conditions that amounted to forced servitude. Some five thousand Native Californians and early settlers are buried in the adjacent cemetery, their names recorded in the mission registers that remain the oldest written documents in San Francisco.

The ornate basilica next door was added in 1918; the old adobe chapel can be visited separately. The contrast between the two buildings — one humble and ancient, one baroque and confident — tells something about what the city thought of its own origins at different moments in its history.
