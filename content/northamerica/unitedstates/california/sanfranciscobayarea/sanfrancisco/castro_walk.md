The Castro has been many things in its hundred and fifty years: a working-class Irish neighbourhood, a Scandinavian enclave, a place where gay men arrived from across the country in the 1970s and made something entirely new. What they built here — a neighbourhood where being openly gay was not a political statement but simply a fact of daily life — changed American culture permanently, at a cost that is still being counted. This walk moves through that history block by block, from the rainbow crosswalks at Castro and 18th Street to the roof deck of a bar where you can look out over the city they remade.

Start at **Harvey Milk Plaza**, the broad triangular plaza at the top of the Castro Street Muni station at Castro and Market. The station entrance is marked by one of the largest rainbow flags in the world, raised on a dedicated pole installed in 1997. The flag was designed by Gilbert Baker in 1978 at Harvey Milk's request — Milk wanted a symbol for the community's first Gay Freedom Day march. The rainbow crosswalks at the intersection of Castro and 18th Street, one block south, were repainted and formalised in 2014; they are now a civic landmark as much as a political one. Stand at the intersection and take in the scale of what was built here: the bars, the shops, the bathhouses converted into gyms, the community organisations occupying storefronts. This neighbourhood was assembled deliberately, by people who had spent their lives told they did not belong anywhere.

Walk one block south on Castro Street to **the Castro Theatre** at number 429. The theatre was built in 1922 by the Nasser family, who had been operating cinemas in the neighbourhood since 1910, and the architect Timothy Pflueger gave it an elaborate Spanish Colonial Revival facade — terracotta ornament, cartouches, a curved parapet — that has become the visual signature of the street. The interior is one of the finest surviving movie palaces in California: a Spanish baroque auditorium with a coffered ceiling, a mezzanine, and the Mighty Wurlitzer organ that rises from the orchestra pit before evening screenings, playing show tunes while the audience sings along. The theatre hosts film festivals, live performances, and repertory screenings; it is the community's great hall as much as its cinema. If you walk past while it is open, go inside.

Continue south on Castro Street. At number 575, there is now a camera shop — or there was one, under different management, when it mattered most. **Harvey Milk's Castro Camera** occupied this address from 1973, and the shop became the de facto headquarters of the neighbourhood's political life. Milk arrived from New York in 1972 with little money and a gift for retail theatrics, and the shop became the place where people came when they needed something done: a zoning variance blocked, a landlord challenged, a campaign organised. Milk ran for the San Francisco Board of Supervisors three times before winning in 1977, becoming one of the first openly gay elected officials in the United States. On 27 November 1978, Supervisor Dan White entered City Hall through a basement window and shot Mayor George Moscone and then Harvey Milk. Milk was forty-eight years old. The vigil that assembled at Castro and Market that night — ten thousand people walking in silence from the Castro to City Hall — was one of the largest spontaneous public mournings in the city's history.

The AIDS crisis that arrived in the early 1980s hit the Castro with particular force. The neighbourhood had built its identity around visibility and community, and it responded to the epidemic through the same mechanisms: organising, fundraising, demanding that a government indifferent to gay lives change its policies. The **NAMES Project AIDS Memorial Quilt** was founded in San Francisco in 1987 by Cleve Jones, who had been a protégé of Harvey Milk. Jones conceived the quilt after noticing that protest placards bearing the names of the dead, taped to the walls of the San Francisco Federal Building, looked like a patchwork blanket. The first panels were displayed on the National Mall in Washington DC in October 1987; the quilt now contains more than 110,000 individual panels and is the largest piece of community folk art in the world. Portions of it are displayed regularly in the Castro.

On 18th Street, turn east from Castro and walk a few doors to the **GLBT Historical Society Museum** at number 4127. The museum holds the largest archive of LGBTQ history in the United States — documents, photographs, ephemera, community records from the 1940s to the present — and its permanent exhibition is the most rigorous account available of how the Castro became what it became. The rotating exhibitions draw on the archive in ways that consistently surprise even people who think they know this history. The museum is small and the admission is modest; it deserves at least an hour.

Continue east on 18th Street to **Midnight Sun** at number 4067. The bar opened in 1971 and is one of the older surviving establishments on the strip; it operates as a video bar, with screens showing music videos and film clips, a format that dates to the pre-internet era when seeing a gay-coded image on a screen was itself a kind of freedom. A few doors further east, at 4049, **Moby Dick** has been open since 1976, making it one of the oldest continuously operating gay bars in the Castro. The interior is unchanged in all the ways that matter: pool table, fish tank, the comfortable shabbiness of a place that does not need to perform anything for anyone.

Return west along 18th Street to Castro and turn north. At the corner of Castro and 17th Street stands **Twin Peaks Tavern**, opened in 1972. Before Twin Peaks, gay bars kept their windows frosted or painted over — a practical measure in an era when being seen in a gay establishment could cost a person their job or their liberty. Twin Peaks was the first gay bar in the United States to install floor-to-ceiling plate glass windows opening directly to the street. The act was deliberately political: it said that the people inside had nothing to hide and no intention of hiding it. The bar was designated a San Francisco landmark in 2013. It tends toward an older clientele and keeps hours that reflect that, opening in the afternoon.

Walk north on Castro Street and then continue along **Market Street**, heading northeast toward Upper Market. Market Street in this stretch is the seam between the Castro and the broader neighbourhood fabric of the city: coffee shops, vintage clothing, the remnants of a hardware culture that served the neighbourhood when it was still building itself. The street climbs gently, and the view back southwest toward Twin Peaks opens as you walk.

At 2369 Market Street, **The Cafe** occupies a corner space on the second floor above street level. It opened in 1986 as one of the few lesbian bars in the city — a correction to a neighbourhood that, in its early years, was more visibly gay male than it was representative of the full range of queer life. The bar has evolved over the decades into a mixed space with a dance floor and a deck; it remains one of the livelier establishments on the upper Market corridor, and one of the few in the Castro with a view of the street below.

Continue northeast on Market Street to **Look Out Bar** at 3600 16th Street, at the corner of 16th and Noe. The bar's roof deck is the destination: a panoramic terrace with a view that takes in the downtown skyline, the Mission, Potrero Hill, and on clear days the hills of the East Bay beyond. It is the kind of view that makes the scale of San Francisco comprehensible — the compactness of the city, the way the neighbourhoods press against each other, the bay framing everything to the east. The bar beneath is straightforward and unpretentious; the roof is why you come.

If the afternoon allows, **Corona Heights Park** is a ten-minute walk north from here, up Roosevelt Way. The rocky summit — volcanic chert, raw and unlandscaped — offers the finest unobstructed view of the Castro from above: the grid of streets, the dome of the Castro Theatre, Market Street running diagonally through the neighbourhood toward downtown. The park also contains the Randall Museum, a natural history museum aimed at children but open to anyone who wants to see the collection of live raptors in the courtyard.

The walk is approximately 1.5 km from Harvey Milk Plaza to Look Out Bar and takes two hours or more at a pace that allows for stops — longer if you spend time in the GLBT Historical Society Museum, which you should. The neighbourhood repays slow attention. It was built by people who understood that presence was itself an argument, and the argument is still being made.
