The stretch of 24th Street running from Valencia Street east to Potrero Avenue is officially recognised as the Calle 24 Latino Cultural District — one of the first state-designated Latino cultural districts in California. The designation, formalised by the Board of Supervisors in 2014 after years of community campaigning, was intended to give the neighbourhood tools to resist the commercial displacement that had already reshaped Valencia Street and was moving south.

The 24th Street corridor has been the commercial and cultural heart of Mission Latino life since the 1940s. On a single block you can still find a Mexican bakery that has been in the same family for decades, a carnecería, a botanica selling religious candles and herbal remedies, a record shop specialising in norteño and cumbia, and three taquerías whose owners have been arguing about whose burritos are better for as long as anyone can remember. The street hosts the annual Carnaval parade in late May, one of the largest Latino cultural festivals on the West Coast, and Día de los Muertos in early November, when the street fills with altars and processions.

The 24th Street Mission BART station marks the end of this walk. The station plaza, designed by Hertzka & Knowles when BART opened in 1973, has been the scene of housing protests, immigrant rights vigils, and neighbourhood celebrations. The entrance features public art including hummingbird-themed metal railings and a tile mural by Jos Sances and Daniel Galvez. Look for the murals on the buildings immediately surrounding the plaza — this intersection has as high a concentration of political street art as anywhere in the neighbourhood.
