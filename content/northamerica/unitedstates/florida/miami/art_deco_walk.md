In 1979, when a one-square-mile patch of derelict South Beach was listed on the National Register of Historic Places as the Miami Beach Architectural District, most of the country thought the preservationists were deranged. The buildings on Ocean Drive were run-down 1930s hotels occupied by elderly Jewish retirees on fixed incomes, their pastel paint long since faded to institutional white. The land was worth far more than the structures on it, and the developers knew it. What followed was one of the great preservation battles in American urban history, driven largely by one woman — Barbara Capitman, who co-founded the Miami Design Preservation League in 1976 and spent the rest of her life chaining herself to buildings slated for demolition. She died in 1990, just as the revival she had engineered was beginning to transform the very neighbourhood whose human scale she had loved. This walk covers roughly 2.5 kilometres from the southern end of Ocean Drive to Lincoln Road, following the arc of that story through eight buildings that survived the wrecking ball and emerged, repainted and reassigned, as the most recognisable streetscape in Florida.

---

The walk begins at the **Park Central Hotel** at 640 Ocean Drive. Henry Hohauser designed it in 1937, and at seven stories it remains one of the tallest buildings on the strip — an outlier in a district that generally kept its hotels low and horizontal. Stand across the street and take in the whole facade: the eyebrow ledges that project over each window like raised brows, the porthole windows near the upper floors, the gently curved corners that soften what would otherwise be a plain rectangular block. These were not decorative choices. The eyebrow ledges cast shade on the glass below, reducing heat before air conditioning existed. The porthole windows fit the nautical motif that many South Beach architects pursued, consciously or otherwise, as the appropriate metaphor for a city built on a sandbar surrounded by water. Hohauser did not invent these features, but he refined them to a pitch of elegance that other architects in the district rarely matched. The hotel's nickname — the "Blue Jewel" of Ocean Drive — has something to do with the shifting color of the stucco in the late afternoon light.

Two blocks north, at 736 Ocean Drive, **the Colony Hotel** makes a different kind of argument. Where the Park Central is imposing through height, the Colony impresses through precision. Hohauser designed it in 1935, when the streamline style was just arriving in South Florida, and the building was among the first on the strip to use steel corner windows — a technology that allowed the continuous, uninterrupted glass curves that give the streamline aesthetic its name. But the Colony's real signature is its neon sign: an inverted-T marquee centered on the facade, spelling out the hotel's name in letters that glow baby blue and navy against the night. Hohauser's original architectural drawings described it as a "unique design," and in 1935, an illuminated neon marquee built into the structure itself rather than tacked on as an afterthought was genuinely unusual. The sign has been photographed so many times it has become a kind of shorthand for the entire district. At night, with the neon reflected in the pavement after rain, it is difficult to argue with the assessment.

---

At 804 Ocean Drive, **the News Cafe** opened in 1988, at a moment when Ocean Drive was still shabby enough to feel almost abandoned after dark. Mark Soyka's concept was straightforward: a cafe, bar, and international newsstand, open around the clock, with tables extending to the sidewalk. It worked faster than anyone expected. Within a few years the place was drawing four to five thousand customers a day — different crowds cycling through in different shifts, from early-morning locals with espresso to sunburned tourists to photographers with models to clubgoers looking for somewhere to sit down at 3 a.m. The cafe became the social center of the renaissance, the spot where the South Beach of the 1990s — the supermodel decade, the nightclub decade — transacted its daily business. Its most faithful regular was Gianni Versace, who walked over each morning from his mansion three blocks north to collect his newspapers. On July 15, 1997, he had just done exactly that and was returning home when he was shot on his front steps. Soyka later said the morning of the murder was the only morning in the cafe's history when there was nothing to read: every newspaper in the rack was about the same story. The News Cafe closed in 2021 and reopened in 2023. Order a coffee and sit outside. The parade on the sidewalk, if somewhat noisier, is not so different from what it was.

Three blocks north, **Casa Casuarina** at 1116 Ocean Drive sits behind iron gates that have not changed since the morning of the murder. The building was constructed in 1930 for Alden Freeman, a Standard Oil heir who modeled it on the Alcazar in Santo Domingo — Mediterranean Revival at its most elaborate, all carved stone and bougainvillea. Versace bought it in 1992 and spent years expanding it, adding a 54-foot pool tiled with 24-karat gold mosaic, covering the walls with hand-painted murals, filling the rooms with antiquities. His investment transformed the house into something between a private villa and a Baroque museum. The contrast between its Mediterranean opulence and the austere Deco geometry of everything around it is still striking. The gates are now the address of a luxury hotel, but the point of stopping here is not the hotel — it is the gate itself, the specific geometry of the front steps, the small plaque. After the murder, South Beach lost some of its innocence about what its revival had become: a playground for an international elite, not a neighbourhood.

---

Cross Ocean Drive and walk east into **Lummus Park**, the mile-long strip of grass between the Art Deco facades and the dunes. The Lummus brothers donated this land to the city in the 1910s, and it has been public ever since — which is precisely why it matters. In the decades when the Art Deco buildings were at their most neglected, the park was the living room of the Jewish retiree community that occupied the cheap hotels. Exercise classes met under the palms each morning. Old men played cards at the concrete tables. On Friday evenings, because hotel rules did not permit open flames in guest rooms, it was common to find pairs of Shabbat candles burning on trays in the lobbies facing the park. Ocean Drive was called "God's Waiting Room" by the people who lived there, without bitterness. These residents — many of them Holocaust survivors, most of them on small fixed incomes, most of them speaking Yiddish among themselves — were the accidental custodians of the buildings Barbara Capitman would later declare worthy of preservation. Their continued presence during the long years of neglect was what kept the developers from demolishing the district before anyone noticed what was there. Walk to the water's edge and turn back: from the beach, the whole pastel parade of Ocean Drive reads as a single unbroken composition, 800 metres of 1930s architecture framed by sky and water.

---

Double back across the park, continue north, and turn west onto 10th Street toward Collins Avenue. The **Essex House** at 1001 Collins is the district's most theatrical nautical gesture. Albert Anis completed it in 1938 and pushed the ocean-liner metaphor to its limit: porthole windows across the upper floors, pronounced horizontal banding, eyebrow ledges so deep they cast genuine shadow, and above everything a narrow vertical tower that rises like a ship's smoke funnel, its neon sign running up the spine in red. The building was designed before air conditioning, before cars dominated the street in front, before the whole urban logic of the neighborhood changed. It was designed, in effect, for pedestrians looking up at it from the sidewalk — and that is still how it works best. Leonard Horowitz, the industrial designer who in the early 1980s developed the now-famous pastel color palette for South Beach, chose shades that would emphasize exactly these kinds of details: the ledges, the curves, the vertical tower. He wanted visitors to see what was already there. The Essex House is the building that most rewards that kind of looking.

Walk half a block west on 10th Street to reach **The Wolfsonian–FIU** at 1001 Washington Avenue. The building itself — a 1927 Spanish Renaissance storage facility — is the first sign that something unusual is happening: the elaborate carved portal and wrought-iron bas-reliefs look nothing like the Deco facades on Ocean Drive a block away. Mitchell Wolfson Jr. began filling this building with his collection in the 1970s, initially renting storage units; by 1986 he owned the building and was committed to making the 200,000-object collection available to scholars. He opened it to the public in 1995 and donated it to Florida International University two years later. The collection spans 1850 to 1950 — propaganda posters, industrial design, political ephemera, appliances, architectural drawings, furniture from the most convulsive century in human history. It is one of the stranger and more intellectually serious museums in the United States. The permanent collection galleries on the upper floors display objects in thematic groupings that would not embarrass a major European institution. Set aside at least two hours.

---

Return east to Ocean Drive and continue north to the **Cardozo Hotel** at 1300 Ocean Drive. Hohauser designed it in 1939, the year after the Essex House, and where the Essex asserts itself vertically, the Cardozo spreads wide and low. The rounded corners and sweeping horizontals give the facade the smooth, fender-like curves of a 1930s American automobile — Hohauser was consciously borrowing from automotive design, which had adopted streamlining for the same practical and aesthetic reasons the architects did. The keystone trim — porous dyed limestone quarried in South Florida — catches the afternoon light differently from stucco and gives the building a warmth that photographs rarely convey. The hotel was named after Supreme Court Justice Benjamin Cardozo, one of the first Jewish justices on the court — a gesture that spoke directly to the community around it. Gloria Estefan, who grew up visiting the neighbourhood with her grandfather, bought the Cardozo in 1994, making it one of the earliest celebrity-backed restoration projects in the district. The restoration was careful and the building looks as it should. The Cardozo was also the first in the district to be fully preserved — which is to say, Barbara Capitman lived to see it happen, just barely.

The walk ends at **Lincoln Road**, four blocks north, where Morris Lapidus banished cars in 1960 and created what was then one of the country's first true pedestrian malls. Lapidus — who also designed the Fontainebleau and Eden Roc hotels to the north — laid out the road with gardens, fountains, shelters, and an amphitheater in the MiMo (Miami Modern) style he had virtually invented. "A car never bought anything," he said, and the remark captures the whole philosophy. Today Lincoln Road is occupied by shops and restaurants in both directions, noisy and crowded at all hours. The Colony Theatre near the western end, restored and reopened as a performing-arts venue, is the best reason to linger. Sit at one of the outdoor tables with a coffee and consider what it means that this walk exists at all: a derelict strip of hotels that should have been demolished in the 1970s is now one of the most visited urban precincts in the Western Hemisphere. Barbara Capitman died practically broke. The buildings are still standing.
