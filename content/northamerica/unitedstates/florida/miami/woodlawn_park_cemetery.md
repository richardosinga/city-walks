Woodlawn opened in 1913 as a garden cemetery on the western edge of what was then still a small city. It became, over the course of the 20th century, the burial ground of the Cuban exile generation's leadership — the place where the men and women who built Little Havana were carried when they died waiting to go home. Carlos Prío Socarrás, who served as Cuba's last democratically elected president before the Batista coup of 1952, is buried here. So is Gerardo Machado, the dictator Prío's predecessors overthrew, which says something about the cemetery's catholicity. The most visited grave is that of Anastasio Somoza Debayle, the deposed Nicaraguan dictator assassinated in Asunción in 1980, who was brought here at the request of Miami's exile community. The grounds are quiet and well kept, the trees large and providing deep shade. Reading the inscriptions on the Cuban section — the dates of birth in Havana or Santiago or Camagüey, the dates of death in Miami — you understand what it means to die in a city that was never your city, waiting for a crossing that never came.
