The Lummus brothers sold their oceanfront property between 6th and 14th Streets to the city of Miami Beach in the 1910s, and the mile-long strip of grass between Ocean Drive and the dunes has been public ever since. In the 1950s through the 1970s, this was where the Jewish retirees who occupied the Art Deco hotels lived their outdoor lives — exercise classes gathered under the palms in the morning, old men played cards at the tables, and on Friday evenings, when hotel rules forbade lighting Shabbat candles in guest rooms, it was common to find pairs of candles burning on trays in the lobbies facing the park. Ocean Drive was known locally as "God's Waiting Room." The designation was affectionate. These residents — many of them Holocaust survivors who had come to South Beach because it was warm, cheap, and had a Yiddish-speaking community — were the quiet custodians of the buildings Barbara Capitman was fighting to preserve. Without them simply living there during the years of neglect, the developers would have had the whole block long before the preservationists arrived. Cross the park to the beach and look back: from the water's edge, the whole pastel parade of Ocean Drive reads as a single composition.
