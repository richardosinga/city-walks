Versailles opened in 1971, founded by Felipe Valls Sr. from Santiago de Cuba, and has operated continuously since as the most politically charged restaurant in Miami. The dining room is large and gaudy in the way of Cuban cafeterias — mirrored walls multiplying the tables, etched glass, chandeliers that cast the room in a warm, slightly theatrical light. The food is Cuban: palomilla steaks, ropa vieja, black beans and rice, croquetas at the ventanita counter outside where you can get an espresso and a pastelito without sitting down. The ventanita is where the real politics happen. When Fidel Castro fell ill in 2006, the international media set up cameras outside and pointed them at the sidewalk, waiting for the exile community to react. When Castro died in November 2016, people poured into the street here — not to mourn but to celebrate a death that many had waited fifty years to witness. U.S. presidents and presidential candidates have all made the pilgrimage to have their photograph taken here. It was awarded an American Classic prize by the James Beard Foundation in 2001. The food is good, the prices are reasonable, and the history is impossible to ignore.
