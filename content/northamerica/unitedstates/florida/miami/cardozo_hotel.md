Henry Hohauser designed the Cardozo in 1939 and named it after Benjamin Cardozo, one of the first Jewish justices on the U.S. Supreme Court — a pointed political gesture in a district whose residents were largely Jewish. The building is Hohauser at his most fluid: where the Park Central is vertical and imposing, the Cardozo stretches wide and low, its rounded corners and horizontal banding giving it the fender-smooth curves of a 1930s Studebaker. The keystone trim — porous dyed limestone quarried from South Florida — catches the afternoon light differently from stucco and gives the facade a warmth that photographs rarely capture. Gloria Estefan, who grew up visiting South Beach with her grandfather, once pointed across the street at the hotel and told him she would buy it one day; she did, in 1994, making it the first building in the district to be restored under private celebrity ownership. The Cardozo was also the first Art Deco building in the district to undergo full preservation restoration. The small plaque beside the entrance is easy to miss, but the story behind it is the story of the whole neighbourhood.
