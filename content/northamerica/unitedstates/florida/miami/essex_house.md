The Essex House at 1001 Collins Avenue is perhaps the purest expression of the Nautical Moderne variant of South Beach Deco — a style that pushed the ocean-liner metaphor as far as it would go. Albert Anis completed it in 1938, and the result is a building that looks genuinely seaworthy: porthole windows run across the upper floors, the main facade is ruled by sharp horizontal lines like the decking of a ship, and a thin vertical tower rises from the roofline like a smoke funnel, its neon sign flickering in red at night. The eyebrow ledges are exaggerated here, almost theatrical in their protrusion. The building makes no attempt to blend into the street — it asserts itself. When Leonard Horowitz arrived in the early 1980s and began repainting the district's buildings in pastel shades of peach, mint, and lavender, the Essex House's nautical details emerged from years of institutional white paint like something being revealed rather than invented.
