The park is named for Máximo Gómez y Báez, the Dominican-born general who commanded Cuban forces in the wars of independence against Spain in the 1870s and 1890s — a figure revered across the Caribbean as a symbol of anti-colonial resistance. In Miami, that name was chosen with care. The park functions as the social heart of Little Havana: a small, fenced enclosure off Calle Ocho where men gather most days to play dominoes at shaded concrete tables, drink Cuban coffee brought from the ventanita across the street, and smoke cigars whose smoke drifts out through the chain-link in a visible signal that the neighborhood is awake and arguing. For years the park had an informal rule: no players under 55. The idea was that the tables belonged to the old men who had built the exile community from nothing, and the young could find their own places. The rule is no longer enforced, but the culture persists — this is not a tourist performance, it is how people actually spend their afternoons. A painted mural of a traditional Cuban street scene runs along the interior wall.
