The Wolfsonian occupies the former Washington Storage Company building on Washington Avenue — a 1927 Spanish Renaissance pile that Mitchell Wolfson Jr. began filling with his collection in the 1970s. By 1986, his objects occupied 90 percent of the storage space, so he bought the building and eventually opened it to the public in 1995. The collection — now over 200,000 objects — focuses on the period 1850 to 1950: propaganda posters, industrial design, political ephemera, architectural drawings, appliances, furniture. It is one of the strangest and most stimulating museums in the United States, a private obsession scaled up to institutional size. The building itself is worth the visit: the lobby entrance features elaborate wrought-iron bas-reliefs and a towering Baroque portal that sits in jarring, productive contrast to the Deco facades lining the rest of the block. Wolfson donated the collection to Florida International University in 1997 — the same year Versace was shot two blocks away — which gives the building an oddly elegiac feeling, as if someone was preserving the century at exactly the moment the century was slipping away.
