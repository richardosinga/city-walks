The Jordaan is usually presented as Amsterdam's most beautiful neighbourhood: the stepped gables, the small canals, the hofje courtyards behind unmarked gates. That is all true. This walk covers the same streets but starts with the stories that the beauty tends to obscure — the painter who died broke in a tattoo studio, the plague cemetery under the hofje garden, the two riots on the same street fifty years apart.

Start at [Rozengracht 184](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/rozengracht_184). The building currently houses a tattoo studio.

!!! story
    This is the address where Rembrandt van Rijn spent the last eleven years of his life. He moved here in 1658 after bankruptcy forced the sale of his grand house on the Jodenbreestraat — the house that is now the Rembrandt House Museum, visited by hundreds of thousands of people a year. At Rozengracht 184, his partner Hendrickje Stoffels died in 1663. His son Titus died in 1668. A year later, Rembrandt died here too, at 63. A notary catalogued the estate: 50 items, including 26 unfinished paintings. He was buried in a rented pauper's grave at the Westerkerk — visible if you look east along Rozengracht toward the tower. No one knows exactly where in the churchyard.

Walk east along Rozengracht, cross the Prinsengracht, and turn north. Bloemgracht — the Flower Canal — is two short blocks ahead. At [Bloemgracht 87, 89, and 91](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/de_drie_hendricken), three nearly-identical stepped-gable houses stand in a row, built in 1642 by a carpenter named Hendrick Roelofsz. Each has a carved gable stone above the door: a townsman, a farmer, a sailor — the three pillars of the Dutch economy. They are called De Drie Hendricken, the Three Henrys, after their builder. What the gable stones don't tell you: this canal was once one of the most industrially active stretches of the Jordaan. At least 14 sugar refineries operated here in the Golden Age. Willem Blaeu, the cartographer whose atlases defined the known world for European navigators in the 17th century, had his workshop on this canal from 1635. The maps that charted the coasts of Africa, Asia, and the Americas were printed a few doors down from where you are standing.

Continue north through the cross-streets into the Jordaan interior. On Egelantiersgracht, look for the gate at number 107. This is the [Sint Andrieshofje](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/sint_andrieshofje). Step through the gate and a covered passage leads into a small courtyard: whitewashed walls, a garden, complete quiet. The hofje was built in the 17th century — privately funded by a wealthy merchant for the city's poor, mostly widowed women, who received a room in exchange for good conduct and regular attendance at church. The merchant's name is on the entrance. The names of the women who lived here are not recorded anywhere. The courtyard has barely changed in 350 years.

A few minutes north, on Karthuizersstraat, is the entrance to [Karthuizerhof](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/karthuizerhof). The hofje guides usually describe it as a masterpiece of 17th-century urban architecture — which it is. City architect Daniël Stalpaert built it in 1650, providing 91 dwellings for widows of sailors and merchants. Stalpaert also designed the Royal Palace on Dam Square. You can walk into Karthuizerhof between 10am and 5pm.

!!! story
    What the guides mention less often: the site was a Carthusian monastery until 1566, when Protestant mobs smashed Catholic churches across the city during the iconoclasm. After the monastery was demolished, the site served as a plague cemetery from 1602. Stalpaert built the courtyard and its 91 dwellings directly on top of the graves. The garden is immaculate.

Continue north to [Lindengracht](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/lindengracht_riots), a wide street in the northern Jordaan.

!!! story
    In 1886, a police officer cut the rope at an eel-pulling contest here — a street entertainment in which a live eel was suspended over the canal and teams competed to pull it free, while the eel, by panicking, provided most of the sport. The authorities had banned eel-pulling as cruel; the neighbourhood considered it a harmless tradition. Cutting the rope started three days of fighting between residents and police that killed 26 people. The episode entered history as the Palingoproer — the Eel Riots.

    Forty-eight years later, the same street came to the barricades again. In 1934, the Dutch government cut unemployment benefits during the Depression. Jordaan residents tore up cobblestones, dismantled bridges to seal off the neighbourhood, and held the streets for several days. On 5 July, a 26-year-old metalworker named Jan Gerressen was shot through the forehead on Lindengracht. Five people died in the Jordaan Riots, possibly more. There is no plaque on Lindengracht for either event.

The walk ends at Noordermarkt, the square in front of the [Noorderkerk](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/things_to_do/noorderkerk). On the corner of the square, there is a small bronze sculpture — the Jordaan Oproer monument, commemorating the 1934 riots. It is modest and easy to miss. On Monday mornings the flea market fills the square; on Saturdays a farmers' market. The neighbourhood continues exactly as it always has.

The Jordaan is often described as charming, and it is. It is also a neighbourhood built in part on plague dead, that housed its most famous painter until he died with nothing, and that twice in fifty years sent residents into the street to be killed. These facts coexist with the beauty of the canals and the quiet of the hofje courtyards. The Jordaan does not ask you to choose between them.

The walk is approximately 1.5 kilometres and takes about an hour at a comfortable pace, longer if you visit the Sint Andrieshofje and Karthuizerhof courtyards.
