Oud-West was built in the last two decades of the 19th century, when Amsterdam's population was doubling every generation and the city was sprinting west across the polders to keep up. The neighbourhood has always been slightly less famous than its neighbours — less grand than the canal ring, less mythologised than the Jordaan, less architecturally dramatic than De Pijp. What it has instead is range: within a 45-minute walk you can move from a Victorian industrial complex being reinvented for the third time, through streets of late 19th-century working-class housing still doing exactly what they were built for, into the most elaborately expressive social housing Amsterdam has ever built. The story of how a city decides what its working districts should look like is legible here in a way it is not quite anywhere else.

Begin at [De Hallen](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/de_hallen), the former municipal tram depot on Kinkerstraat. The building was completed in 1902 by the engineer W.C. Metzelaar, and its function was absolutely prosaic: tram cars were serviced here, and the hall was designed to hold as many as possible under one roof. The structure is iron and brick, the roof a series of barrel vaults supported on cast-iron columns at close spacing. Trams ran through the depot until 1996. Stand in the main hall and look at the roof: the vaults are brick with the ironwork exposed and painted, and the whole thing is approximately as beautiful as a Victorian railway terminus, which is to say considerably more beautiful than anything built for transit infrastructure in the last forty years. The building has been converted into a food and flea market, a cinema, a hotel, and a library. It is, as far as possible, a completely ordinary neighbourhood amenity — which is the correct use of a building like this.

Head north on Kinkerstraat and turn west on to [Hugo de Grootplein](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/hugo_de_grootplein), where you will encounter the beginning of a different era. The housing blocks around this square were completed between 1909 and 1914 — early Amsterdam School, before the movement reached its full expressive ambition. The architects working here — mainly Piet Kramer and Johan van der Mey, often in collaboration or competition — were experimenting with what a housing block could do that a conventional *revolutiebouw* tenement could not. Look at the corner buildings on the square: the rooflines break and step. Decorative panels appear not as applied ornament but as integral brick patterns — the same material as the wall, worked into a different texture. The building at the south-east corner has a series of panels at the third-floor level where the brickwork shifts from bond to a herringbone pattern for no structural reason, purely to mark the change. This is the key Amsterdam School move: ornament that comes from the building rather than being stuck on to it.

Continue north and west along [Da Costakade](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/da_costakade_school), the canal that marks the western edge of Oud-West proper before it gives way to the Westerpark area. The housing along the west bank of Da Costakade was built primarily between 1908 and 1920, and you can follow the development of the Amsterdam School style building by building — the earlier blocks more restrained, the later ones progressively more free in their use of curved surfaces, recessed brickwork, and elaborate ironwork at the balconies and entrances. Notice how the street-level façades break at irregular intervals: there is almost no straight line of identical windows for more than 25 metres, because the architects were resisting the repetition that *revolutiebouw* had made legible. They were expensive buildings for working-class families to live in, and the city housing authority spent considerable political capital defending the cost. The counterargument — that people who live in places designed with intention live better — was Michel de Klerk's most consistent position.

From Da Costakade, follow the canal north into the [Westergasfabriek](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/westergasfabriek) complex, the former municipal gasworks. The Westergasfabriek was built in stages from 1883, when Amsterdam's gas supply still came from coal, and the architects — working for a municipal utility rather than an aesthetically minded client — nonetheless produced buildings of considerable ambition. The main purifier building is a steel-and-glass structure whose structural logic is entirely exposed, closer to an English Victorian railway station than to any contemporary Dutch model. The gasometer frame — the skeletal steel cylinder that once held the gas holder — still stands at the northern edge of the site, now a shell enclosing a performance space. The conversion of the complex into restaurants, bars, event venues, and park began in the late 1990s and has been handled with more restraint than such conversions usually manage: the industrial character of the buildings has been preserved, the additions are distinguishable from the original fabric, and the park around them is a serious park rather than a shopping development with grass. It is one of the better examples of post-industrial regeneration in the Netherlands.

From the Westergasfabriek, walk east into the [Spaarndammerplantsoen](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/spaarndammerplantsoen) and the streets around it. This is the Spaarndammerbuurt, built almost entirely between 1913 and 1923 by the Amsterdam School's principal architects — de Klerk, Kramer, and Van der Mey — for the socialist housing cooperative De Arbeiderswoning. The neighbourhood is an outdoor museum of the movement at its most ambitious and most confident. The housing blocks here are larger than anything in Oud-West proper, and the architects were working with a client whose brief was not to maximise density but to demonstrate that working-class housing could be beautiful. Every block has a distinct character. Some are organised around interior courtyards. Others use the corner as a compositional statement — towers rising, rooflines swooping, the whole mass of brickwork treated as a sculptural problem. The building at the corner of Hembrugstraat and Zaanstraat, designed by de Klerk in 1917, has a roofline that rises in a long convex curve to a kind of prow at the corner, then drops back in a straight slope — a ship's profile, deliberately nautical in a city built on water.

The walk ends at [Museum Het Schip](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/museum_het_schip), which occupies the most famous building in the Spaarndammerbuurt. De Klerk designed the Het Schip block in 1919, and it is the culmination of everything the Amsterdam School had been moving toward since those first tentative experiments at Hugo de Grootplein. The post office at ground level has survived intact: iron and tilework, curved surfaces, a space so deliberately beautiful that the original city council objected to the use of public funds for a post office whose users were working-class. The museum's apartment reconstruction shows how a family of the period actually lived — two rooms, a shared washhouse, and a view of a wall. Architecture this ambitious, made for housing this modest: that gap is what the Amsterdam School was, and it is worth sitting with for a moment before you leave.

The walk is approximately 3.5 kilometres and takes about 90 minutes at a comfortable pace, longer if you visit the museum.
