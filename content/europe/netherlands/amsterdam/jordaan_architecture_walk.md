The Jordaan was built between 1612 and the 1650s, mostly in one sustained push of construction that left behind a neighbourhood where you can still read 400 years of building history from the pavement. This walk is about learning to read it. By the end you will know how to tell a stepped gable from a neck gable, why Amsterdam houses have a hook projecting from the attic, what a Protestant church looks like when it is trying to be honest about what it is, and what separates a merchant house from a worker's house even when both are made of the same brick. The Jordaan is not one thing. It is a compressed record of who could afford to build what, and when.

Start at the [Westerkerk](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/things_to_do/westerkerk) on Westermarkt — Hendrick de Keyser's masterpiece and the tallest church tower in Amsterdam at 87 metres. De Keyser was the city's most important architect in the early 17th century, and the Westerkerk, begun in 1620 and consecrated in 1631, is his mature statement on what a Protestant church should look like.

Stand back and look at the tower, the Westertoren. The structure is essentially Gothic: a tall stone tower built in the medieval tradition, with buttresses that carry the weight of the walls outward. But the surface is covered in Renaissance ornament — pilasters, cornices, and classical mouldings stacked in decorating bands up the shaft. This mixing of Gothic engineering and Renaissance dress is what de Keyser called "Amsterdam Renaissance." The city never went fully classical in the Italian sense — it adapted the vocabulary to what it already knew how to build. At the very top, the Imperial Crown of Austria sits in blue and gold: a gift from Maximilian I, who granted Amsterdam the right to use it in 1489 as thanks for commercial support.

!!! story
    Rembrandt was buried somewhere in the churchyard here in 1669, in a rented pauper's grave, the exact location now lost. He had moved out of his grand house on the Jodenbreestraat eleven years earlier after bankruptcy; the tower visible from his last address on Rozengracht was likely the last landmark he could name. No one has ever found the grave.

The church itself is built on what de Keyser called a "double Greek cross" plan — two equal-armed crosses connected to form a long nave. The three-aisled basilica, 58 metres long and 29 metres wide, was at consecration the largest Protestant church in the Netherlands. The nave is taller than the aisles; this is the basilica section visible from outside in the stepped roofline. Keep this form in mind — the Noorderkerk at the end of this walk takes a completely different approach.

Cross west over the Prinsengracht into the Jordaan. Head south a short block on Prinsengracht, then turn west on Bloemstraat to reach Bloemgracht — the Flower Canal. This is the walk's first major lesson in gable typology.

At [Bloemgracht 87, 89, and 91](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/de_drie_hendricken), three matching houses built in 1642 stand in a row: the De Drie Hendricken, the Three Henrys, named after their builder Hendrick Roelofsz. These are the finest surviving examples of the **stepped gable** (*trapgevel*) — the earliest and most common type in Amsterdam, fashionable from the late 16th century through about 1660. The stepped gable is exactly what it sounds like: the top of the building ascends in a series of right-angle steps, each face of the step a separate vertical surface, until it reaches a small point or pediment at the peak. Look at the three houses together and notice the symmetry: each gable is divided into the same three bays by pilasters, each step is the same proportion, and each has a carved gable stone above the door — a townsman, a farmer, and a sailor, the three pillars of the Dutch economy as their builder saw it.

The stepped gable is a functional form as well as a decorative one. Amsterdam houses were narrow (often five to seven metres wide) because property tax was levied on street frontage, so builders went deep and tall instead. The gable at the top was the only part of the building that showed its full width to the street — it was where the money went.

Now look up and down Bloemgracht in both directions. You are standing in a canal lined almost entirely with 17th-century buildings — some with stepped gables, some with the later neck gable that superseded it. The stepped gable has those right-angle steps; the neck gable (*halsgevel*), which became fashionable from around 1640 and dominated by 1680, has a narrow rectangular "neck" of wall between two curved flanking scrolls called volutes. Where the stepped gable looks functional and practical, the neck gable looks architectural — it draws on Italian Baroque precedents and announces that the owner has cultural pretensions as well as commercial ones. Many of the houses along this canal show the transition: earlier buildings with stepped tops, later ones with the neck, and occasional examples of the bell gable (*klokgevel*), which rounds the top into a flowing arc.

Bloemgracht was one of the most industrially active streets in the Golden Age Jordaan. At least 14 sugar refineries operated here. Willem Blaeu, the cartographer whose atlases charted the known world for European navigators in the 1630s and 1640s, had his printing workshop on this canal. The houses you are looking at were not the homes of the wealthy — those were on the Herengracht and Keizersgracht — but of the substantial middling sort who made the Jordaan work.

Head north from Bloemgracht on Tweede Leliedwarsstraat, turn east on Egelantiersgracht. At number 107 is the gate of the [Sint Andrieshofje](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/sint_andrieshofje). Step through it.

What you enter is a *hofje* — a courtyard almshouse. The form is entirely functional: a gateway in the street wall, a covered passage, a courtyard surrounded by small one- or two-room dwellings, a garden at the centre. Wealthy 17th-century merchants built these as charitable endowments for the city's poor, usually elderly widowed women, who received a room in exchange for good conduct and church attendance. There are more than 40 hofjes surviving in Amsterdam; the Jordaan has the highest concentration.

The architectural interest is in the silence of the plan. From the street, you have no idea this courtyard exists. The gateway is narrow and unmarked except for the donor's name above it. Inside, the scale drops dramatically — from the four-storey street-front buildings to one- and two-storey courtyard dwellings, from the noise of Egelantiersgracht to complete quiet. This compression of scale within a single urban block is as sophisticated as anything on the canal ring, but it was designed not for display but for withdrawal. The hofje is an architecture of interiority.

Continue north through the cross-streets — Derde Egelantiersdwarsstraat, Madelievenstraat, Anjeliersstraat — into the denser grid of the Jordaan interior. The streets narrow. Pay attention to the houses along these blocks: plain brick fronts, four storeys, paired windows on the upper floors, and at the gable peak a timber beam projecting outward over the street. This is the **hijsbalk**, the hoisting beam, and it is the detail that tells you most about the ordinary Jordaan house.

Amsterdam houses were built without interior staircases wide enough for furniture or goods. Everything large — barrels, bales, wardrobes, sacks of grain — went up the outside, hoisted by rope through the widened upper window, which tilts outward from the top on purpose to keep the load away from the wall as it rises. The staircase hook and rope are a practical solution to a property-tax constraint. You will see hijsbalken on nearly every block; they remain in use today for moving furniture.

Turn east on Karthuizersstraat to reach [Karthuizerhof](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/karthuizerhof). City architect Daniël Stalpaert built this hofje in 1650 — the same Stalpaert who designed the Royal Palace on Dam Square that same decade. Karthuizerhof is the grandest hofje in the Jordaan: 91 dwellings arranged around a large formal courtyard, the entrance flanked by classical pilasters, the whole composition rigorously symmetrical in a way that the Sint Andrieshofje is not.

Compare the two hofjes. Sint Andrieshofje is intimate, almost accidental-looking — a gate in a wall, a passage, a small garden. Karthuizerhof is civic architecture: a formal entrance, a broad courtyard, proportions that recall a cloister or a public institution. Stalpaert was the city's official architect, and he treated this commission accordingly. The difference is between a merchant's private charity and a city project — the same building type, the same social purpose, completely different architectural ambitions.

Walk east along Karthuizersdwarsstraat to [Lindengracht](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/lindengracht_houses). This wide street — wide by Jordaan standards, at least — runs the length of the northern Jordaan and is one of the best places to read the ordinary Jordaan house in volume. Look along both sides of the street. Four storeys. Narrow brick. Hoisting beams at the top. Gables that vary — stepped, neck, some later flat or cornice tops from the 19th century — but the underlying form is always the same: a narrow rectangle as tall as it can go, with all the architectural effort concentrated in the strip between the top-floor windows and the sky.

The repetition is the point. The Jordaan was built fast, by many different builders, but to a shared set of constraints: plot width, building height, structural system of load-bearing brick walls with timber floors. Within those constraints, every builder made slightly different choices about the gable. The gable was the part of the house that was yours — the advertisement, the identity, the investment that distinguished your house from the identical one next door.

Continue east on Lindengracht and then south on Noorderkerkstraat to the [Noorderkerk](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/things_to_do/noorderkerk), which faces Noordermarkt square. Hendrick de Keyser designed this church in 1620, the same year he began the Westerkerk. He died in 1621; his son Pieter completed it in 1623. The two buildings could hardly be more different.

Where the Westerkerk is a basilica — tall central nave, lower side aisles, Gothic vertical structure dressed in Renaissance ornament — the Noorderkerk is a perfectly symmetrical Greek cross. Four arms of equal length project from a central octagonal volume; small annex buildings fill the corners. There is no tower. There is no hierarchy of nave and aisle. Every seat in the church is the same distance from every wall. Look at the exterior: the plan is completely legible from the outside. The four equal gabled arms, the central octagonal roof, the corner annexes — you can read the whole building at a glance.

This was a theological statement as much as an architectural one. The Noorderkerk was built for the working-class population of the Jordaan, who needed a church in their own neighbourhood rather than walking south to the Westerkerk. The perfectly egalitarian plan — no direction more sacred than any other, no nave that orients everyone toward the east — reflects a Protestant theology in which the word, preached from a central pulpit, matters more than the direction of prayer. The Jordaan's workers got an architecturally radical building.

The square outside, Noordermarkt, is where the city's best organic farmers' market operates on Saturday mornings. On Monday mornings, a flea market. The Jordaan has always been a working neighbourhood.

Walk east from Noordermarkt on Prinsengracht and turn north to reach [Brouwersgracht](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/brouwersgracht_warehouses), the canal at the northern boundary of the canal ring. Stand at the water and look west.

Brouwersgracht — the Brewers' Canal, named for the many breweries that operated here in the Golden Age — is the clearest place in Amsterdam to compare the full range of Dutch gable types in a single view. The warehouses (*pakhuizen*) along the north quay are the wide, functional buildings with **spout gables** (*tuitgevels*): plain triangular or rounded tops with no classical ornament, just a hook at the peak for the loading rope. The spout gable is the working gable — built for loading, not for looking at. It is wide because warehouse floors were wide. It is plain because warehouses didn't need to advertise social status.

Between and beside the pakhuizen, the merchant houses announce their owners differently. The neck gable (*halsgevel*) with its pilasters, its carved sandstone coping, its flanking volutes — the curved scrolls that soften the transition between the narrow neck and the wider facade below — is the dominant form by the late 17th century. A neck gable on a canal house says: I know what is fashionable in architecture, I have the money to hire a mason who can carve it, and I am not a warehouseman even if I trade with them. You have walked from stepped gable to neck gable in about two kilometres; you have watched the city's architectural vocabulary change in real time.

The bell gable (*klokgevel*), which appears occasionally in this view and elsewhere in the canal ring, is the late 17th and early 18th century's answer to the neck: instead of a rectangular neck between two scrolls, the top of the building flows in a single curved arc from cornice to peak, like the profile of a bell. It is softer and more Baroque than the neck gable, and it signals a slightly later date and a slightly higher budget.

Brouwersgracht is not technically in the Jordaan — it is the canal ring's northern edge — but it was the Jordaan's backyard, its working waterfront. The view from here closes the walk's argument: you have moved through three centuries of building, from de Keyser's Gothic-Renaissance hybrid at the Westerkerk, through the Golden Age's gable catalogue on Bloemgracht, through the civic and charitable architectures of the hofjes, to the working city's plain pragmatics on the quayside. The Jordaan looks homogeneous from a distance. It is not.

The walk is approximately 2 kilometres and takes about 90 minutes at a comfortable pace, longer if you enter the Sint Andrieshofje and Karthuizerhof courtyards.
