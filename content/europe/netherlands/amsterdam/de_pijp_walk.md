De Pijp is the youngest of Amsterdam's classic neighbourhoods and the most urban in feel. Where the Jordaan was built for craftsmen and the canal ring for merchants, De Pijp was built for everyone else — quickly, cheaply, and densely, in the 1870s and 1880s when Amsterdam's population was expanding faster than the old city could absorb. The result is a grid of narrow streets lined with four-storey brick tenements, their facades barely wider than two windows, their ground floors long since converted into shops, cafes, and market stalls. Today it is one of the most interesting places to walk in the city.

Start at the [Heineken Experience](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/heineken_experience) on Stadhouderskade 78. The large red-brick complex was founded as a brewery in 1867 by Gerard Adriaan Heineken, who bought the failing De Hooiberg brewery on this site and rebuilt it on an industrial scale. For over a century the building produced beer for export across Europe. Production moved to a larger facility outside the city in 1988, and the brewery has been a museum ever since. You don't need to go in — the building is worth a look from the outside, its scale conspicuous among the surrounding residential streets.

From the brewery, head southwest to [Marie Heinekenplein](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/marie_heinekenplein), the small square at the northern end of Ferdinand Bolstraat. The cafes here fill every terrace chair the moment the sun appears. This square marks the threshold: behind you is Stadhouderskade, the wide ring road that separates De Pijp from the older city. Ferdinand Bolstraat begins here and runs south through the heart of the neighbourhood.

Walk south along Ferdinand Bolstraat. This is the main spine of De Pijp, named for the painter Ferdinand Bol, a pupil of Rembrandt. The streets crossing it to the left and right are named for other Dutch painters: Frans Hals, Jan Steen, Jacob van Ruisdael, Gerard Dou. The naming was not affectionate — it was simply the convention for a neighbourhood being built so fast that no one had time to think of better names. The street itself is busy and direct, with shops at ground level and the characteristic four-storey façades pressing close on both sides.

At the bottom of Ferdinand Bolstraat, turn east into [Albert Cuypmarkt](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/albert_cuypmarkt). The market fills Albert Cuypstraat from here all the way to Ceintuurbaan — 300 metres of stalls selling cheese, raw herring, stroopwafels made fresh to order, fabric, spices, electronics, and produce from Suriname, Turkey, and Morocco. It is the largest daytime market in the Netherlands, and one of the few places in Amsterdam where the city's full diversity is visible all at once. Near the western entrance, look for the bronze statue of André Hazes.

!!! story
    Hazes grew up in De Pijp and began singing at this market at the age of eight — his father was violent and barely employed, and he sang for coins to buy his mother a birthday present. On Liberation Day 1959, the comedian Johnny Kraaykamp Sr. heard him here and brought him on national television that same evening. When Hazes died of a heart attack in 2004, at 53, 48,000 people came to Amsterdam Arena to say goodbye.

Walk east through the full length of the market. About halfway along, you reach [Gerard Douplein](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/gerard_douplein), the small square where Gerard Doustraat crosses the market. This is a good place to stop. The market widens slightly here, and looking north along Gerard Doustraat you see the view that defines De Pijp: a straight canyon of brick tenements disappearing into the distance, every ground floor a shop or café, the upper floors solid and uniform. Buy a stroopwafel if you haven't already — the ones made fresh, the caramel still soft, are worth stopping for.

From Gerard Douplein, turn south on Gerard Doustraat and walk into the quieter streets between the market and the park. The noise of the stalls fades quickly. These backstreets — narrower and more residential than Ferdinand Bolstraat — are typical of the older, inner parts of De Pijp: the same four-storey brick buildings, but without the ground-floor commerce, a little more worn, a little more lived-in.

[Sarphatipark](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/sarphatipark) opens up a few minutes' walk south. It was designed in 1885 and named for Samuel Sarphati (1813–1866), a Jewish physician who transformed Amsterdam through public health initiatives, a factory that produced affordable bread for the poor, and grand building projects including the Amstel Hotel. Sarphati died before De Pijp was built; the neighbourhood grew up around his legacy. During the German occupation, the park was renamed Bollandpark in 1942 because Sarphati was Jewish. The old name came back in 1945. The monument at the centre is easy to find.

From Sarphatipark, walk west through the quiet backstreets to [Ruysdaelkade](/europe/netherlands/amsterdam/ruysdaelkade), the canal along De Pijp's southern edge. After the noise of the market, the canal is noticeably still. The canalside houses are well maintained and the water reflects their facades. Jacob van Ruisdael, for whom the canal is named, painted Dutch landscapes in the 17th century: flat horizons, wide skies, water carrying light. The view along the canal has something of that quality.

The walk is approximately 2.5 kilometres and takes about an hour at a comfortable pace, longer if you stop at the cafes on Marie Heinekenplein or walk slowly through the market.
